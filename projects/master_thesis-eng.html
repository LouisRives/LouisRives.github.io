Master Thesis — Does Personality Shape the Tutorial?

◆  SOULS-LIKE  ·  GAME DESIGN  ·  PERSONALITY PSYCHOLOGY  ◆

✦

Match the Player, Not the Game: An Exploratory Study of Personality-Adaptive Tutorial Design in Souls-like Games.

Souls-like games — titles like Dark Souls, Elden Ring, and Lies of P — are known for their punishing difficulty and steep learning curves. Most players encounter the same tutorial regardless of who they are. But what if the way a game introduces itself should depend on the player's personality?

This thesis investigates whether matching tutorial type — minimal versus explicit — to a player's Openness to Experience (a Big Five personality trait) improves their subjective experience during initial gameplay. The study is grounded in Person-Environment Fit theory, Self-Determination Theory, and flow theory, and was conducted as an exploratory 2×2 between-subjects pilot study (N = 16) using Lies of P as the test environment.

Participants were classified as High or Low Openness via the BFI-10 questionnaire and randomly assigned to either a minimal tutorial (discovery-based, genre-typical) or an explicit tutorial (an 8-page supplementary PDF guide covering stamina mechanics, combat systems, and boss encounter expectations). Three subjective outcomes were measured post-gameplay: enjoyment, perceived competence, and intention to continue playing.

No statistically reliable interaction effect was found across any outcome, and no effect survived Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate correction — as expected for a pilot of this scale. However, a directionally coherent and theoretically interesting pattern emerged in perceived competence: Low Openness players reported substantially lower competence under the Explicit Tutorial than the Minimal Tutorial (M = 3.33 vs 6.00), while High Openness players were unaffected by tutorial type (M = 4.20 in both conditions). This direction runs contrary to the naive prediction — and may point toward a standard-setting mechanism whereby explicit guidance raises a performance benchmark that makes failure feel more personally damning.

The study establishes proof-of-concept for the methodology, identifies perceived competence as the outcome most worth investigating in a powered replication, and contributes the first empirical data point on personality-tutorial interaction in Souls-like games.
