Master Thesis — Does Personality Shape the Tutorial?

◆  SOULS-LIKE  ·  GAME DESIGN  ·  PERSONALITY PSYCHOLOGY  ◆

✦

Match the Player, Not the Game: An Exploratory Study of Personality-Adaptive Tutorial Design in Souls-like Games.

Souls-like games — titles like Dark Souls, Elden Ring, and Lies of P — are known for their punishing difficulty and steep learning curves. Most players encounter the same tutorial regardless of who they are. But what if the way a game introduces itself should depend on the player's personality?

What I built. I turned that question into a real player study. I designed two onboarding experiences for Lies of P — a minimal, discovery-led version and an explicit 8-page combat guide — measured each player's personality with a validated Big Five questionnaire, then ran 29 players through 90-minute sessions up to the first boss, capturing both how they played and how they felt afterwards (enjoyment, sense of competence, intent to keep playing).

What I found. The headline result was counterintuitive: players handed the detailed walkthrough felt less capable than players left to figure things out for themselves. More guidance didn't help — it seems to set a performance bar that makes early failure sting more. Personality didn't cleanly predict who benefited from which tutorial, which pointed to a concrete design takeaway: a single trait is too blunt a lever for adaptive onboarding. The study didn't confirm its original hypothesis, and reading that honestly — instead of overselling a weak signal — is part of what the work taught me.

What it shows. End to end, the project covered experimental design, player-testing logistics, statistical analysis in R, and translating messy results into design implications — taking a fuzzy design intuition, making it testable, running it with real players, and being straight about what the data can and can't say.
