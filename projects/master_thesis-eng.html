Master Thesis — Does Personality Shape the Tutorial?

◆  SOULS-LIKE  ·  GAME DESIGN  ·  PERSONALITY PSYCHOLOGY  ◆

✦

Match the Player, Not the Game: An Exploratory Study of Personality-Adaptive Tutorial Design in Souls-like Games.

Souls-like games — titles like Dark Souls, Elden Ring, and Lies of P — are known for their punishing difficulty and steep learning curves. Most players encounter the same tutorial regardless of who they are. But what if the way a game introduces itself should depend on the player's personality?

What I built. I turned that question into a real player study. I designed two onboarding experiences for Lies of P — a minimal, discovery-led version and an explicit 8-page combat guide — measured each player's personality with a validated Big Five questionnaire, then ran 29 players through 90-minute sessions up to the first boss, capturing both how they played and how they felt afterwards (enjoyment, sense of competence, intent to keep playing).

What I found. The headline result was counterintuitive: players handed the detailed walkthrough felt less capable than players left to figure things out for themselves. More guidance didn't help — it seems to set a performance bar that makes early failure sting more. Personality didn't cleanly predict who benefited from which tutorial, which pointed to a concrete design takeaway: a single trait is too blunt a lever for adaptive onboarding. The study didn't confirm its original hypothesis, and reading that honestly — instead of overselling a weak signal — is part of what the work taught me.

What it shows. End to end, the project covered experimental design, player-testing logistics, statistical analysis in R, and translating messy results into design implications — taking a fuzzy design intuition, making it testable, running it with real players, and being straight about what the data can and can't say.

◆  WHAT DEVELOPERS CAN TAKE FROM IT  ◆

Beyond the headline result, the discussion turns the findings into practical guidance for anyone designing onboarding:

Front-loaded text tutorials are the worst format.
Dumping mechanics on players right before they play overloads working memory at the exact moment they need it free. Just-in-time tooltips and in-context prompts at first encounter teach the same content without the cognitive tax.
Players are poor judges of what helped them.
In playtests they reliably rate the more thorough tutorial as "better" — even when it left them performing worse and feeling less competent. "Was the tutorial helpful?" measures what was covered, not what actually worked.
Measure competence, not satisfaction.
Track signals that don't depend on the player's opinion — time to first checkpoint, retries before progressing, damage taken in the first fight, how much of the area they explored. These predict whether players keep going far better than tutorial ratings do.
Adaptive onboarding should read behaviour, not ask questions.
A pre-game personality quiz gets gamed (players pick whatever yields the shorter tutorial) and few players tolerate it. Inferring play style from the first few minutes — who reads versus skips, explores versus rushes — can't be gamed and needs no questionnaire.

◆  THE BIGGER QUESTION  ◆

The most useful way to read the study isn't "the hypothesis wasn't confirmed" — it's that a widely held design intuition produced the wrong prediction. Almost every commercial tutorial assumes more guidance makes a more confident, more capable player. Here the data leaned the other way, and the more-guided players still rated their guidance as more appropriate. They believed it helped; it didn't. That gap — between what players think helped them and what actually did — is the part worth carrying beyond Souls-likes.

If a reasonable prediction about onboarding can be this wrong, what else might be? The stakes climb outside games: anywhere a learner's own confidence is part of the outcome — a junior clinician, a new driver, a pilot trainee — training that's rated highly but quietly leaves people less capable than they feel is dangerous precisely where competence matters most. One pilot study can't prove this generalises, but it sharpens a question the training literature has largely skipped: satisfaction with training and the actual effect of training are not the same measurement, and they may diverge more than we assume.
